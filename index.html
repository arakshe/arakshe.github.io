Startup Funding Visualizations

Group Members: Anvi Rakshe, Manika Sakulsureeyadej, Mehek Daga

Project Description: These visualizations explore funding round patterns and sources among startups using interactive diagrams and charts.

About the Sankey Diagram

This Sankey diagram visualizes the flow of startups through different stages of funding rounds—grouped into 1–3 rounds, 4–6 rounds, and 7+ rounds—and how these stages connect to various funding sources. The diagram reveals that venture capital is the dominant funding source across all stages, particularly in the early rounds.

Startups in their early phases often rely on angel investors, seed funding, and convertible notes. As companies mature through additional funding rounds, they begin to diversify their funding sources, increasingly turning to private equity, debt financing, grants, and post-IPO options.

About the Bar Chart

This bar chart illustrates the number of startups falling into each category of funding rounds (1–3, 4–6, and 7+ rounds). It helps contextualize the Sankey diagram by showing the distribution of startups across different maturity stages.

From the chart, we can observe that the majority of startups receive funding within the first 3 rounds, with a steep drop-off as they move into mid- and late-stage funding. This reflects both the challenges of sustained fundraising and the natural attrition of startups over time.

The visualization provides a high-level overview of startup lifecycle progression, supporting deeper insights into how and when companies secure their capital.

About the Box Plot

The box plot visualization uses D3.js to display the distribution of funding amounts across various rounds or categories. It includes medians, quartiles, and outliers to give a statistical overview of funding variability.

This chart provides insight into not just how many startups raise funds, but the scale and spread of funding they receive. Outliers reveal exceptionally high or low funding events, which can highlight unique opportunities or risks.
